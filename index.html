Good Faith in International Contracting

Nature of Negotiation

Contract negotiation: pre-contractual process to define terms of a binding agreement. Parties are free to negotiate and withdraw, but not to act arbitrarily.

Key Dynamics

Negotiation involves strategy, information asymmetry, and reliance. Legal systems regulate negotiations to prevent opportunistic conduct.

I. Principle of Good Faith

Core Guiding Principle

Civil Law Tradition

General principle governing negotiations from inception. Mandatory duty of honesty and loyalty throughout negotiation process.

Common Law Approach

No general duty to negotiate in good faith. Preference for certainty and specific rules. Protection through estoppel and misrepresentation.

Functional Definition

Honesty and loyalty adapted to context. Requires transparency, consistency, and protection of reasonable expectations.

Historical & Economic Foundations

  • Common Law / Equity Divide: Historical tension between strict legal rules and equitable correction. Case-by-case development of fairness principles through judicial discretion.
  • Liberal Economic Model: Contract as expression of rational self-interest. Freedom of negotiation central to market efficiency. Good faith limited by autonomy principle.
  • Indirect Survival Mechanisms: Good faith survives indirectly through estoppel, misrepresentation doctrines, and unconscionability. Contextual application rather than general duty.

Good Faith & Negotiation Theory

ZOPA & BATNA Framework

Zone of Possible Agreement

No duty to reach agreement even if ZOPA exists. Good faith protects the legibility of ZOPA. Parties must not deliberately obscure or manipulate boundaries.

Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement

BATNA justifies freedom to withdraw. Parties may exit when alternatives are more favorable. Bad faith arises from contradictory or misleading conduct.

II.B. Critical Analysis & Limits

Limits of Good Faith

  • No Unified Definition: Criticized as vague "catch-all" moralistic notion with variable content across systems, undermining predictability.
  • Tension with Autonomy: General duty may be incompatible with divergent interests and confidentiality obligations inherent in negotiations.
  • Difficult Proof: Proving bad faith in informal exchanges increases judicial discretion and risk of casuistic decisions.
  • Cultural Differences: Negotiation styles vary significantly across jurisdictions, complicating uniform application of good faith standards.
  • Power Imbalance: Principles imperfectly protect weaker parties lacking resources for enforcement through arbitration.

08

Conclusion

Regulatory Tool

Good faith = regulatory tool, not moral ideal. Balances freedom, efficiency, and trust in commercial negotiations.

Protection Mechanism

Protects ZOPA & BATNA without forcing agreement. Enables withdrawal while preventing opportunistic conduct.

Future Challenges

AI & artificial information asymmetry. Algorithmic negotiation and automated contracting raise new good faith questions.

Global Harmonization

Continued development through UNIDROIT, CISG, and international arbitration practice.
